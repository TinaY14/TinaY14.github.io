title: Volunteering in the Teaching Kitchen: We Tried Fried Bananas! 
date: 2026-03-04

This week in the kitchen, we made fried bananas, which was a totally new experience for me. I usually just eat bananas raw for my daily fruit intake, or maybe mash them up into pancake batter, but I had never actually tried frying them before. For my taste, the heavy frying process actually ended up masking the natural sweetness of the banana. Still, I'm glad that I tried something new with bananas - but that side dish made from pineapple and bean mix was very delicious. I loved the flavor of it so much, I'd be more than happy to just make a bowl of that on its own!

title: Golden, Crispy, and Safe: Lessons from the Potato Pancake Kitchen
date: 2026-02-18
image: images/021826/p3.jpg,images/021826/p1.jpg,images/021826/p2.jpg,images/021826/p4.jpg
excerpt: Stepping into the teaching kitchen this week, where we made potato pancakes!
---

This week’s teaching kitchen was very interesting, we made potato pancakes!

Before we started the session, Laura asked to give each of our participants one layered thick gloves and one food service glove on our dominants hands to protect against the sharp potato peelers. It was a simple precaution, but it made everyone feel safe and confident while prepping. 

Frying the potato pancakes in oil turned them a very beautiful golden brown, and the savory aroma of frying potatoes almost filled the east dining hall. They smelled so good that almost everyone passing by couldn't help but look over. The pancakes themselves had a slightly salty taste.

We experimented with two very different dips: a savory cream cheese and a sweet pineapple sauce. While the pineapple added a fun, sweet twist, I still recommend trying the cream cheese, I think it really elevated the savory flavor of the potatoes.For a final touch, a sprinkle of cinnamon on top made them look gourmet and added a surprisingly delicious flavor.

The biggest lesson for today is that the size of the pancakes really matters! We finished our batch really quickly because we learned a kitchen secret: making the pancakes thinner and smaller means they cook much faster. I actually watched another student try to cook one massive, thick pancake. It took so long, and unfortunately, the outside edges completely burned before the inside had a chance to cook through. It was so charred that he couldn't even salvage a bite and had to toss the whole thing. Lesson learned: keep them thin, and you'll get that perfect crunch every time!

title: No-Cook Guacamole from the Teaching Kitchen
date: 2025-09-17
image: images/091725/g.jpg,images/091725/g2.jpg,images/091725/g3.jpg
excerpt: Volunteering in the Teaching Kitchen, we made fresh guacamole!!

---

I love eating chips with Guacamole! 

Avocados, the main ingredient in Guacamole, have a pretty stellar reputation for good reason. We all know they're packed with good fats, great for heart health, and helpful for keeping blood pressure in check. They're also very filling that I often see friends who eat them as a staple for weight management. Plus, they're loaded with vitamin E and antioxidants, which is a really nice bonus for our skin.

Honestly, my favorite way to eat an avocado will always be in guacamole. There is just nothing better than scooping up guacamole with a salty potato chip. That creamy, mild flavor of the avocado balances out the crunch and salt of the chip perfectly.

Even though I’ve eaten chips with guacamole countless times, I had never really stopped to think about how it was actually made. Today, Laura walked us through the whole process, and I think the best part is just how easy it can be made. There’s no need to turn on a stove, it all comes down to fresh ingredients: you just toss them in a bowl, mash them up, mix them well, and you instantly have a fresh, nutritious dip.

What made today’s batch very special, though, were the tomato cuties. They didn't come from a grocery store like other ingredients, they were grown right here in our school’s garden!
I actually got to help pick them during a recent gardening group GBM. Seeing that crossover between the gardening group and the nutrition club was very rewarding. There is something really satisfying about planting food in a small campus garden and seeing it directly provide fresh, nutritious ingredients for students to cook with in the teaching kitchen session. 

What a perfect full-circle moment!

title: Pedaling for Smoothies: My Food Harvest Volunteer Experience
date: 2025-11-12
image: images/111225/s1.jpg,images/111225/s2.jpg,images/111225/s4.jpg,images/111225/s3.jpg,images/111225/s5.jpg,
excerpt: Making 200+ pedal-powered smoothies at the Food Harvest event was a busy but unforgettable volunteer experience!

When I got to the volunteer stand for the Food Harvest event, it started with a quick supply run. Kelsey took me over to the school kitchen, and we used a truck to haul out boxes of frozen pumpkin chunks, frozen bananas, and oat milk that had been prepped the night before. The smoothie bike was already set up with clean blenders waiting for us. Kelsey showed me the ropes, and the whole process was actually super simple. All I had to do was add a scoop of the frozen pumpkin and banana, pour in the oat milk, pop the blender back onto the bike, and secure it with an elastic band. Then, people could just hop on and pedal. It blended up really fast, taking under five minutes to get a perfect smoothie.

I was the only one managing the station, wearing my apron and gloves, trying to encourage passing students to hop on and blend their own drinks. Managing three blenders, getting the ratios right, and serving it all up kept me on my toes. Most people were in a rush, just grabbing a cup and hurrying off to class, but it was so fun watching groups of curious friends try the bike while teasing each other. They would pedal so happily, taking pictures for their new experience.

My favorite moment of the morning involved a girl on roller skates. After finishing what was amazingly her seventh cup, she looked at me and asked if we really didn't add anything else to make it taste so good. When I told her the only ingredients were pumpkin, banana, a little cinnamon, and oat milk, she was very surprised that something so simple could be so tasty and nourishing. She actually ended up staying with us for a while, helping to invite other people to join in. It was really cool to make a new friend like that. Even the people who were rushing off often came back for a second cup once they realized how delicious it was.

It was a busy three hours, but very satisfying. We went through over 200 cups and had barely any left by the end of the event. After everything was over, putting the clean stand back exactly how we found it gave me a great feeling of accomplishment. It was a completely new experience for me, and I really cherish the memory of that fun, fast-paced morning!!
 
I was so busy blending and serving that I sadly didn't get a chance to snap any photos myself. The pictures here are actually from Laura, taken during a different smoothie bike event!

This morning, I had the chance to visit the fermentation laboratory at Jiangnan University, and it was honestly such a cool experience. Walking in, I was immediately attracted by the sheer number of machines and the complex setup they have for studying different brewing techniques. It was spectacular to see it all in person and get a behind-the-scenes look at how things actually work in their fermentation lab.
What stood out to me the most was the intense level of precision involved. I've always thought of brewing as more of a traditional craft, but seeing their highly precise tools somehow changed my perspective. For the first time, I understood how exact these brewing experiments have to be and how complex each experiment is. It takes a high amount of testing and scientific control to consistently produce good wine and achieve big technological breakthroughs. Seeing how advanced the technology has become was interesting, and it gave me a clearer understanding of the science of fermentation. 
When I shared researchers with the results of my own homemade rice wine project (which I had been dealing with a frustrating issue where my rice wine was growing mold with little black dots), they gave me some guidance, explaining that the room where I fermented it probably just wasn't clean or sterile enough. The happiest moment was when I heard that despite the surface mold, the rice wine underneath actually fermented really well! Getting that professional feedback really gave me a huge boost of confidence. I walked away with clear next steps to fix my environment, and I'm so excited to make my next batch even better!

title: A Tour to the Lingzhi Factory: Exploring the Ganoderma!
date: 2023-07-05
image: images/070523/l.jpg,images/070523/l1.jpg,images/070523/l2.jpg,images/070523/l3.jpg,images/070523/l4.jpg,images/070523/l5.jpg,images/070523/l6.jpg,images/070523/l7.jpg,images/070523/l8.jpg,images/070523/l9.jpg,images/070523/l10.jpg,images/070523/l11.jpg,images/070523/l12.jpg,images/070523/l13.jpg,images/070523/l14.jpg,images/070523/l15.jpg,images/070523/l16.jpg,
excerpt: Visiting a massive Ganoderma processing plant really opened my eyes to the mountain cultivation and amazing uses of this unique and multi-purpose mushroom.

Today, dad took me to a tour of a massive Ganoderma lucidum processing plant, which was quite a cool experience. The facility itself was huge, with promotional videos playing on big screens as we walked through. After we arrive, the staff offered us some Ganoderma water to try. It was a new flavor for me: it tasted a little sour and bitter at first, but it left a surprisingly sweet aftertaste once you got used to it.

Ganoderma, also known as lingzhi, grows mostly in southeast China, where provinces like Fujian, Guangdong, and Guangxi are home to a huge variety of them. Over 66 different kinds across the country, coming in colors like red, blue, yellow, white, and even with black spots. According to traditional practices, the red ones are often used to support lung health, while the purple ones are said to be good for sleep and are even used as an ingredient in makeup foundation (Isn't this cool? How did our ancestors know that?). The factory also highlighted many traditional benefits, explaining how lingzhi has historically been used to help lower blood pressure, protect the heart, boost immunity, and promote healthy aging.

The specific factory I visited was producing Ganoderma lucidum spore oil, and learning about how they actually grow it was interesitng. We learned that the environment is important, which is  why lingzhi cultivated high up in the mountains. They transport lingzhi logs up the mountain, cover them with soil, and grow  directly on the wood. It’s a very strict process requiring tissue separation, mycelial proliferation, and a lot of permits and quality control. The growers have to constantly monitor for the perfect temperature and humidity, while managing insect removal, tissue testing, and preventing contamination. To get the best yield, they prune the side branches so all the nutrients concentrate on the main stem, allowing it to grow vigorously. What suprised me was when hearing that the mushroom actually has self-repairing properties, but as it ages, it eventually hardens into a woody texture.

When the plant reaches membrane differentiation and produces a lot of powder, that's when it is time to harvest. In order to collect the spores, which mature slowly with distinct white edges, they use plastic covers to isolate the mushroom from the soil. Once harvested, the spores go through a low-temperature physical process to break their cell walls, followed by oil extraction, hydraulic pressing, and canning. Lingzhi really is a multi-purpose plant. They use thick slices of the mushroom directly, while the powder gets turned into everything from capsules and medicines to beverages and face masks. Seeing their experimental research and development lab during this tour was the best part: when talking to the researchers, I saw their eyes were filled with passion for what they do.

I've toured a few food factories before, but this one really stood out to me. I didn't know much about Ganoderma lucidum before this trip, and I never realized how many potential benefits it has. Growing up in the south, I’m no stranger to seeing different kinds of plant spores in nature, but seeing the careful cultivation of lingzhi was novel. It still feels like a pretty rare ingredient in the everyday market, and I’m hoping to explore more ways to use it in my future research by chance!
