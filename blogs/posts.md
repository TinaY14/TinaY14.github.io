title: Golden, Crispy, and Safe: Lessons from the Potato Pancake Kitchen
date: 2026-02-18
image: blogs/images/potatopancakes/p1.jpg
excerpt: Stepping into the teaching kitchen this week, where we made potato pancakes!
---

This week’s teaching kitchen was very interesting, we made potato pancakes!

Before we started the session, Laura asked to give each of our participants one layered thick gloves and one food service glove on our dominants hands to protect against the sharp potato peelers. It was a simple precaution, but it made everyone feel safe and confident while prepping. 

Frying the potato pancakes in oil turned them a very beautiful golden brown, and the savory aroma of frying potatoes almost filled the east dining hall. They smelled so good that almost everyone passing by couldn't help but look over. The pancakes themselves had a slightly salty taste.

We experimented with two very different dips: a savory cream cheese and a sweet pineapple sauce. While the pineapple added a fun, sweet twist, I still recommend trying the cream cheese, I think it really elevated the savory flavor of the potatoes.For a final touch, a sprinkle of cinnamon on top made them look gourmet and added a surprisingly delicious flavor.

The biggest lesson for today is that the size of the pancakes really matters! We finished our batch really quickly because we learned a kitchen secret: making the pancakes thinner and smaller means they cook much faster. I actually watched another student try to cook one massive, thick pancake. It took so long, and unfortunately, the outside edges completely burned before the inside had a chance to cook through. It was so charred that he couldn't even salvage a bite and had to toss the whole thing. Lesson learned: keep them thin, and you'll get that perfect crunch every time!

title: Mindful Eating: A Beginner's Guide
date: 2025-02-10
image: images/placeholder3.jpg
excerpt: Learn how slowing down and being present during meals can transform your relationship with food.
---

In our busy lives, eating often becomes just another task to check off our list. We eat at our desks, while scrolling through our phones, or rushing between activities. But what if we approached eating differently?

Mindful eating is about bringing full attention to the experience of eating. It's noticing the colors, smells, textures, and flavors of your food. It's recognizing hunger and fullness cues. It's eating without judgment.

Start small: try eating one meal a day without distractions. Put away your phone, turn off the TV, and simply focus on your food. Notice how it makes you feel. You might be surprised by what you discover.

title: Fermented Foods: Ancient Wisdom for Modern Health
date: 2025-01-20
image: images/placeholder4.jpg
excerpt: Discovering the gut-health benefits of fermented foods and how to incorporate them into your diet.
---

Fermented foods have been part of human diets for thousands of years, from Korean kimchi to German sauerkraut, from Japanese miso to Indian lassi. Our ancestors knew instinctively what science is now confirming: these foods are incredibly beneficial for our health.

The fermentation process creates beneficial bacteria (probiotics) that support our gut microbiome. A healthy gut is linked to better digestion, improved immunity, and even mental health benefits.

Easy ways to add fermented foods to your diet: start your day with yogurt or kefir, add sauerkraut or kimchi to your meals, enjoy kombucha as a refreshing drink, or try miso soup as a warming starter.

title: Hydration Myths Debunked
date: 2025-01-05
image: images/placeholder5.jpg
excerpt: Separating fact from fiction when it comes to how much water you really need to drink.
---

You've probably heard the advice to drink eight glasses of water a day. But where did this number come from, and is it actually backed by science? The truth might surprise you.

Our hydration needs vary greatly depending on our size, activity level, climate, and diet. Many foods, especially fruits and vegetables, contain significant amounts of water that contribute to our daily intake.

The best indicator of proper hydration is actually quite simple: the color of your urine. Pale yellow means you're well hydrated. Listen to your body's thirst signals - they're usually quite reliable. And remember, coffee and tea do count toward your fluid intake despite the caffeine!

title: The Rainbow Diet: Eating by Color
date: 2024-12-18
image: images/placeholder6.jpg
excerpt: Why filling your plate with colorful produce is one of the simplest ways to improve your nutrition.
---

Nature has given us a beautiful guide to nutrition: color. The vibrant hues of fruits and vegetables aren't just visually appealing - they represent different phytonutrients that offer unique health benefits.

Red foods like tomatoes and watermelon contain lycopene. Orange and yellow foods are rich in beta-carotene. Green vegetables provide chlorophyll and folate. Blue and purple foods offer anthocyanins. Each color brings something special to your plate.

Challenge yourself this week: try to eat at least three different colored vegetables each day. Not only will your meals look more appetizing, but you'll be giving your body a wider range of nutrients.

title: Meal Prep Made Simple
date: 2024-12-01
image: images/placeholder7.jpg
excerpt: Practical tips for preparing healthy meals in advance without spending your entire weekend in the kitchen.
---

The biggest obstacle to healthy eating isn't knowledge - it's time. When we're tired and hungry, convenience wins. That's where meal prep comes in, and it doesn't have to be as overwhelming as social media makes it seem.

Start small: prep just one component, like cooking a big batch of grains or chopping vegetables for the week. These building blocks make assembling healthy meals much faster on busy weeknights.

My favorite time-saving strategy? Cook once, eat twice. Make double portions of dinner and pack the leftovers for tomorrow's lunch. It's simple, reduces food waste, and ensures you have a nutritious meal ready to go.

title: Understanding Food Labels
date: 2024-11-15
image: images/placeholder8.jpg
excerpt: A guide to decoding nutrition labels and ingredient lists so you can make informed choices at the grocery store.
---

Food labels can feel like they're written in a foreign language. What does "natural" really mean? How do you interpret the percent daily values? And why are ingredients listed in that specific order?

Here's a key insight: ingredients are listed by weight, from most to least. So if sugar appears in the first three ingredients, that product is likely high in added sugars - even if it's marketed as healthy.

Focus on the serving size first, as all the numbers are based on it. Pay attention to fiber (higher is usually better), sodium (lower for most people), and the types of fats. Ignore most front-of-package claims - the real information is in the Nutrition Facts panel.

title: Sleep and Nutrition Connection
date: 2024-10-28
image: images/placeholder9.jpg
excerpt: How what you eat affects your sleep, and how your sleep affects what you eat - a fascinating two-way relationship.
---

We often think of sleep and nutrition as separate aspects of health, but they're deeply interconnected. Poor sleep can increase cravings for high-calorie foods, while certain foods can either help or hinder your sleep quality.

Foods containing tryptophan (like turkey, nuts, and seeds), magnesium (leafy greens, whole grains), and complex carbohydrates can support better sleep. On the flip side, caffeine, alcohol, and heavy meals close to bedtime can disrupt your rest.

Try to finish eating at least 2-3 hours before bed. If you need a bedtime snack, keep it light - a small handful of almonds or a banana with a little nut butter can actually promote better sleep.
